Population Growth and Classical and Neoclassical Economic Growth theories
within the context of Burundi's Economic development

*Noe Vyizigiro
B.A. in Economics, Middlebury College, Middlebury, VT.
Email: noevyizigiro8@gmail.com
January 22, 2024

Photo by Mashaliki TV.

The population of Burundi has been astronomical, with a growth rate of 2.7% as of 2022. The population has doubled since 2001 and is expected to double again by 2050 if nothing is done and assuming a constant rate of growth. Despite this, people's incomes have been declining, resulting in a loss of purchasing power as the costs of goods and services increase. As a result of lack of capital accumulation and technological advancement, Burundi is heavily reliant on subsistence agriculture, which supports approximately 90% of the population. There has been a decrease in agriculture production in proportion to the growth of the population, resulting in food insecurity, which has contributed to the stagnation of economic growth in the country.
To analyze the impact of demographic growth on economic development, this study uses secondary data from the World Bank time series and the DHS 2016/2017. We use Vector Error Correction Model and Linear OLS regression model for the time series and linear and nonlinear probability models for the fertility preference analysis using DHS data. The study finds a significant negative impact of population growth on both cereal production per capita and real GDP per capita in the short-run and in the long-run. Regarding peopleâ€™s fertility preference, we find that people prefer fewer children than they actually have, with the ideal number of children being between 3 and 4. Despite the study not assuming that only population growth is the major cause of Burundi's economic slippage, it argues that population control targeting the maximum of 3 children per woman, agricultural reform, and a free and competitive market, would have a significant impact on Burundi's socio-economic development.
